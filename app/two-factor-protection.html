Two Factor Protection

Add an extra layer of security to your accounts with Two-Factor Authentication (2FA).

What this is

This section explains Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) and guides you through enabling it on your important accounts, emphasizing the use of strong second factors like authenticator apps or hardware keys over SMS.

Plain definition

A security method requiring two different types of verification to prove your identity, typically something you know (password) and something you have (phone, hardware key).

Why it matters

Even with a strong, unique password, accounts can still be compromised through phishing or malware. 2FA acts as a critical second barrier, preventing unauthorized access even if your password is stolen.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

If a hacker steals your password, without 2FA, they can log right into your account. With 2FA enabled, even with your password, they would still need the second factor – for example, a code from your authenticator app or access to your physical security key – which they don't have. This significantly reduces the chance of a successful breach. SMS-based 2FA is less secure as SIM-swapping attacks can bypass it.

Common mistakes written as warnings

  • Warning: Relying solely on SMS for 2FA, which can be vulnerable to SIM-swapping.
  • Warning: Not saving recovery codes, which can lead to permanent account lockout if you lose your second factor device.
  • Warning: Forgetting to enable 2FA on your password manager, which is a critical oversight.
