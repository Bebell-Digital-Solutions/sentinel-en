Email as Identity

Separate your primary email from online services to reduce risk.

What this is

This section guides you on how to use different email addresses for different purposes, especially separating your critical accounts (banking, government, primary communication) from less important online services (shopping, newsletters, forums).

Plain definition

Using multiple email addresses to compartmentalize your digital identity and minimize the impact of a single email compromise.

Why it matters

Your email address is often the key to resetting passwords and accessing many of your online accounts. If a less secure service you signed up for with your primary email gets breached, your primary email becomes vulnerable, potentially exposing all linked accounts.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

Imagine using your main email for a new online store. That store suffers a data breach, and your email address is leaked. Now, spammers target your primary inbox, and attackers might try to use that email to reset passwords on your banking or social media accounts. By using a separate "junk" email for such services, you contain the risk to that less critical address.

Password Strategy

Develop a robust strategy to create and manage unique, strong passwords.

What this is

This section provides guidance on how to create strong, unique passwords for all your online accounts and how to manage them effectively, typically using a password manager.

Plain definition

A systematic approach to generating and storing complex, distinct passwords for every online service you use.

Why it matters

Password reuse is one of the biggest security risks. If one service you use is breached, and you've reused that password elsewhere, attackers can easily gain access to all your other accounts.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

Imagine you use "MyDogName123!" for your social media, email, and banking. If the social media site is hacked and your password is leaked, criminals will immediately try "MyDogName123!" on your email and banking accounts. A strong password strategy prevents this domino effect, making each account an independent fortress.

Two Factor Protection

Add an extra layer of security to your accounts with Two-Factor Authentication (2FA).

What this is

This section explains Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) and guides you through enabling it on your important accounts, emphasizing the use of strong second factors like authenticator apps or hardware keys over SMS.

Plain definition

A security method requiring two different types of verification to prove your identity, typically something you know (password) and something you have (phone, hardware key).

Why it matters

Even with a strong, unique password, accounts can still be compromised through phishing or malware. 2FA acts as a critical second barrier, preventing unauthorized access even if your password is stolen.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

If a hacker steals your password, without 2FA, they can log right into your account. With 2FA enabled, even with your password, they would still need the second factor – for example, a code from your authenticator app or access to your physical security key – which they don't have. This significantly reduces the chance of a successful breach. SMS-based 2FA is less secure as SIM-swapping attacks can bypass it.

Scam and Fraud Awareness

Learn to recognize and avoid common online scams and fraud attempts.

What this is

This section educates you on various types of online scams, phishing attempts, and fraudulent schemes, providing practical tips to identify and avoid them. It focuses on developing a skeptical mindset towards unsolicited communications.

Plain definition

Understanding the tactics used by fraudsters to trick you into revealing personal information or sending money, and developing habits to protect yourself.

Why it matters

Scams and fraud are constantly evolving. Being aware of common techniques helps you protect your finances, identity, and personal data from malicious actors who exploit trust and urgency.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

A common phishing scam involves an email that looks like it's from your bank, asking you to "verify your account" by clicking a link. If you click and enter your credentials on the fake site, attackers steal your login information. Another example is a tech support scam where a pop-up claims your computer is infected and asks you to call a number, leading to remote access and demands for payment. Recognizing these patterns is your first line of defense.

Inbox and Account Cleanup

Reduce your digital footprint by cleaning up old accounts and managing your inbox.

What this is

This section guides you through identifying and closing unused online accounts, unsubscribing from unwanted newsletters, and generally reducing the number of digital access points that could be exploited.

Plain definition

Minimizing your online presence by deactivating dormant accounts and decluttering your digital communications to reduce potential attack surfaces.

Why it matters

Every online account you have, especially those you no longer use, represents a potential vulnerability. If an old, forgotten account is breached, your associated email or other personal data could be exposed without your knowledge.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

You might have signed up for a forum or an old e-commerce site years ago and forgotten about it. If that site suffers a data breach, your old username and password (which you might have reused) could be leaked. This provides attackers with information they can use to try and access your active accounts. Cleaning up these dormant accounts removes these hidden risks.

Tracking, Cookies, Ads

Take control of your online privacy by managing tracking, cookies, and personalized ads.

What this is

This section explains how websites and advertisers track your online activity using cookies and other technologies, and provides actionable steps to minimize this tracking and reduce personalized advertising.

Plain definition

Implementing browser settings and tools to limit how companies collect and use your online behavior data for advertising and profiling.

Why it matters

Extensive online tracking can lead to a loss of privacy, manipulation through targeted advertising, and your data being shared or sold to numerous third parties without your full understanding or consent.

Real risk explanation with concrete examples

When you visit a shopping site, cookies remember what you viewed. Later, you might see ads for those exact products on other websites or social media. This is targeted advertising based on tracking. More concerning, this data can build a detailed profile of your interests, habits, and even vulnerabilities, which can be used for purposes beyond simple advertising. Taking control means limiting who can build these profiles.
